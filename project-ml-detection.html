Overview

Oceanic fronts are boundaries between water masses with different properties and play crucial roles in marine ecosystems and biogeochemical cycles. Traditional detection methods rely on manual analysis or simple gradient techniques. This project developed a deep learning approach using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for automated detection and segmentation of oceanic fronts from satellite observations in the Bay of Bengal.

Research Objectives

  • Develop a CNN-based semantic segmentation model for oceanic front detection
  • Create labeled training dataset from satellite SST and chlorophyll imagery
  • Compare model performance with traditional gradient-based methods
  • Analyze seasonal and spatial patterns of front occurrence in the Bay of Bengal

Training & Validation

Created labeled dataset of 500+ satellite images with manually annotated front locations. Used 70-15-15 split for training, validation, and testing. Evaluated model performance using precision, recall, F1-score, and Intersection over Union (IoU) metrics.

Key Findings

Model Performance

  • Accuracy: Achieved 87% precision and 82% recall on test dataset
  • IoU Score: Mean Intersection over Union of 0.74, indicating strong spatial overlap
  • Comparison: Outperformed traditional gradient-based methods by 23% in F1-score
  • Robustness: Model performed well across varying cloud cover and sea state conditions

Oceanographic Insights

  • Seasonal Variability: Front occurrence peaks during monsoon transition periods
  • Spatial Patterns: Fronts concentrated along shelf break and river discharge plumes
  • Physical Mechanisms: Strong correlation with freshwater discharge and upwelling events
