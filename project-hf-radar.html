Overview

This project focuses on characterizing surface current patterns in the northern Gulf of Mexico using High-Frequency (HF) radar systems from the WAVCIS network. The research aims to develop improved methodologies for current field analysis and understand the temporal and spatial variability of shelf circulation.

Research Objectives

  • Analyze long-term HF radar datasets to identify current patterns and circulation features
  • Develop quality control procedures for HF radar velocity measurements
  • Investigate seasonal and event-driven variability in shelf currents
  • Validate radar observations with in-situ measurements and model outputs

Methodology

Data Collection

The WAVCIS network operates multiple CODAR SeaSonde HF radar stations along the Louisiana coast. These systems measure surface currents by detecting the Doppler shift of radio waves scattered by ocean waves. We utilize both radial velocity data from individual stations and combined vector current fields.

Analysis Techniques

• Quality control using signal-to-noise ratio and geometric dilution of precision (GDOP) metrics
• EOF analysis to identify dominant circulation modes
• Spectral analysis for temporal variability characterization
• Comparison with ADCP measurements and numerical model outputs

Preliminary Findings

Current Progress

  • Data Quality: Established quality control protocols for HF radar velocity measurements
  • Circulation Features: Identified persistent current patterns associated with shelf break processes
  • Validation: Ongoing comparison with ADCP measurements showing good agreement (R² > 0.75)
  • Publications: Manuscript in preparation for Journal of Physical Oceanography
